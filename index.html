Australian Energy Consumption

How does Australia's Energy Consumption progress over time?

In this section, the line charts show the trend of
Australian Energy Consumption as a whole in absolute and relative
values (Energy consumption per capita), and the choropleth map shows
how much energy is consumed per capita for each state.
Including both Nationwide and Statewise data is a must because
a country following a general trend does not imply all
its states will follow suit.

Although Australian Energy consumption shows an increasing trend in terms
of absolute values, there is a depleting trend in terms
of Energy Consumption per capita (in GJ/person). According to renewedeconomy.com.au,
the notable main factors contributing to the decrease of Energy
Consumption per capita are the influence of energy efficiency programs
and the economy moving away from energy-intensive industries. (Reference this)

If we are scrutinising statewise data, the state with the
most notable change of Energy Consumption per capita is Northern
Territory. Although Northern Territory only has consumed 281.5 Gigajoules of
energy per person in 2003, it has more than doubled
the amount of energy consumed in 2021 at 582.4 Gigajoules
of energy per person, which defies the trend of Australian
Energy Consumption per capita.

Energy Consumption has played out on each Australian state. Drag
the slider to find out more!

How much does Australia consume each type of fuel?

This stacked area chart shows the proportion of the consumption
of each fuel type with respect to the year between
2003 and 2021, inclusive.

In general, Australia's fuel consumption is dominated by Fossil Fuels,
which makes renewable energy consumption numbers insignificant. However,
as time elapses, the proportion of energy consumed by Fossil Fuels has
been declining, as there is an Australian Government Scheme called
the Renewable Energy Target. Its goal is to reduce the electricity sector's
greenhouse gas emissions and enhance electricity generation from sustainable and
renewable sources. (Reference this)

In terms of fossil fuels, Refinery Input was the dominant
energy source in 2003, as 1629.5 Petajoules of Refinery input
was consumed, which is greater than Black Coal consumption on
that year. However, it was dethroned by Black Coal in 2005.
On that year, consumption of black coal is recorded at 1554.4 Petajoules,
compared to Refinery Input's consumption of 1543.7 Petajoules on that
year. Nevertheless, Black Coal's reign as the most dominant fossil fuel consumed
ended in 2011, as Natural Gas regains its throne back as the most dominant
fossil fuel consumed at 1632.8 Petajoules, compared to Black Coal's
consumption of 1402.7 Petajoules on that year.

However, Natural Gas consumption has been increasing steadily over time,
as it became the most dominant fossil fuel being consumed
by Australians in 2015 with 1409.1 Petajoules, and the likelihood of
Natural Gas being the most dominant fossil fuel being consumed
in the future is high because it is the only fossil fuel that shows
an increasing trend.

In terms of the renewables, Wood, woodwaste was the dominant
energy source in 2003, with 105.4 Petajoules of Wood, woodwaste being
consumed at that time. Despite that, it is losing its dominance over
time because the amount of Wood, woodwaste has been following
a declining trend, while other renewable energy sources, such as
Solar Hotwater and Liquid/Gas biofuels has increased its
consumption as time goes by.

This is because in 2003, the amount of Solar Hotwater
consumed in Australia is only 2.8 Petajoules. However, as time
goes by, it eventually broke the 10 Petajoules milestone in 2010,
in which 10.5 Petajoules of energy was consumed, and it eventually
reached 19.7 Petajoules of energy in 2021. On the other hand, the
amount of Liquid/Gas biofuels consumed in Australia was only
11.2 Petajoules in 2003. Furthermore, it broke the 20 Petajoules milestone
in 2010, in which Australians consumed 22.5 Petajoules in 2010. After reaching
that milestone, the amount of Liquid/Gas biofuels consumed in Australia
maintains to be at least 20 Petajoules, but has not reached 30 Petajoules yet.
Therefore, there is potential that Solar Hotwater and Liquid/Gas biofuels
will be the most dominant renewable energy source consumed in the future.

How does the Australian Energy Flow look like?

According to the alluvial diagram shown on the right side,
most of the energy types that Australia used are produced
locally, with a minuscule proportion of imported energy. This is
inevitable because Australia has a vast number of energy reserves.

Firstly, Black Coal is an EDR, which is an Economic
Demonstrated Resource. According to Australian Parliament House,
an Economic Demonstrated Resource is a resource in which profitable
extraction or production under defined investment assumptions is possible. (Reference this)
Most of Australia's Black Coal EDR is dominated by Queensland
and New South Wales. Some of them are located in
Tasmania, Southern Australia, and Western Australia. Some notable sites that
produce Black Coal are the Hail Creek Mine in Queensland,
Bowen Basin in Queensland, which hails the largest coal reserves
in the country, (Reference this) and the Sydney Basin in New
South Wales.

Brown Coal is mainly produced in one state, which is
Victoria, in which Latrobe Valley contributes to more than 90%
of Australia's Brown Coal production. Some notable Brown
coal mines in Australia are the Loy Yang Mine and
the Yallourn Mine. (Reference this) As production of Brown Coal is concentrated
in only one state, it is likely that there would
be less Brown Coal produced than Black Coal

However, Australia does not consume all of its own Natural
Gas and Crude Oil, as it still imports that energy source from
other countries, despite having a handful of Natural Gas reserves and Crude Oil
Refineries in the country. Some notable Natural Gas Reserves in
Australia include Bass Strait in Victoria and Tasmania, Offshore Northwest
shelf in Western Australia, and the Darling Downs in Southern Queensland. (Reference this)

Finally, Renewable Energy production in Australia is entirely produced indigenously,
such as solar hotwater and liquid/gas biofuels. As Australia has an abundance of sunshine,
wind, and natural attributes, it is a great country for producing renewable energy, which
implies that Australia does not need to import renewable energy from other countries. (Reference this)
